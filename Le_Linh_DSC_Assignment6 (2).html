5. Process Summary¶

In order to build a logistic model to predict churn customers for Fitness Center, I followed the follwing steps:

  1. Collect the data: I gather all relevant data from Fitness Center, in this case is quite simple and straightforward. However, if the data is scattered and messy, I will need to load and transform the raw data into one usable dataset.
  2. Preparing the data: I then calculated some statistical descriptions of my input data to identify potential problems and outliers. In this case, I plotted histogram of all variables' and saw that my age column had some very obvious data problems, which I corrected by eliminating those data points and imputing the median age inplace.
  3. Choosing and training the model:
  • A logistic regression was pre-selected, however, I did need to import all the necessary libraries (sklearn and martplotlib) in order to train my model and to visualize my outputs.
  • After I chose to have my training set to be 30% in accordance to the common practice when splitting data into training, validation and test sets.
  • I tested a few combinations of predictors to see which combination will result in the best model performance, and I did find that transforming pmttype into numeric variable and including it in the model makes the model performance significantly better (from an accuracy of 0.84 to 0.91). I also found that including enrolldt in the model will weaken the model's ability to identify default customers (the results being the model labelling all customers to be 'not default'). Therefore, enrolldt is the only variable I eliminated from my model.
  1. Evaluate the model: I used ROC Curve, Confusion Matrix and Gains Table to display and evaluate my model. Per my conclusions after each of the visualization, my model's predictions have a high accuracy, F1 and recall rates, especially for non-default users. This is due to the imbalance in our original dataset (only about 10% is identified as 'default'). Thus, this is a potential area for improvement to better improve our predictions for default customers.

6. Discuss why certain variable expected to be significant are/are not and any other unexpected insights.¶

While calculating the correlations between the predictors and the response, I found that there were a few variables not having a strong correlation with default, such as age and gender. However, when I eliminated age and gender from my model, the model actually performed worse than one that has the two variables. Therefore, I learned that looking at correlations is not the absolute signal to include or eliminate a variable from the model. I also was surprised that pmttype alone accounts for much of the model performance. This insight also verifies my hypothesis, so it is a good insight that is both data-driven and business-sensical that I can use to provide to our client.
